Part B

Holiday Content Strategy

A new content architecture for seasonal and holiday pages — built on the same hub-and-spoke model we're applying to the rest of the site.

The Problem

Blog Posts Don't Compound

Every year we publish a new blog post for Mother's Day, Father's Day, Valentine's Day. Every year, those posts start from zero in search rankings. Google classifies holiday searches like "Mother's Day things to do near me" as recurring queries — it doesn't need a new URL each year. It needs the most authoritative, most recently updated page.

3–6 mo
Time for a new blog post to reach competitive rankings — past the holiday window
~Days
Time for an updated evergreen page to re-rank — it already has authority
Zero
Backlinks, ranking history and engagement signals on a brand-new blog post
Years
Of compounding authority on an evergreen page refreshed annually

An evergreen page updated annually compounds like an investment. A new blog post every year is starting over from scratch.

Three Approaches Compared

ApproachSEO PerformanceMaintenanceCannibalization Risk
New blog post each yearLowest — starts from zero every timeHigh — new post + old post cleanupHigh — old and new posts compete

Competitive Landscape

What Other DMOs Do for Holidays

Most mid-size DMOs — including Visit Fairfax — are still publishing one-off blog posts for holidays. The best-resourced DMOs build evergreen pages. This gap is our opportunity.

Direct Competitor

Our opportunity: Evergreen holiday pages give us a structural advantage they don't have.

Wine Country Parallel

Our takeaway: The wine country positioning means we can follow their playbook directly.

State DMO

Seasonal landing pages that persist year-round and get refreshed. Blog posts handle timely hooks and specific angles. Strong internal linking between blogs and category pages.

Our takeaway: Good example of the hybrid hub-and-spoke model at the state level.

Explore Asheville

Best-in-Class Model

Pillar-cluster model: seasonal content pillars layered on activity hubs. Blog posts feed back into hubs, not standalone content. Organized by persona and season.

Our takeaway: This is the exact architecture we should be building toward.
Visit Fairfax has 3x our hotel supply but no dedicated holiday pages. Building evergreen hubs gives us a structural SEO advantage over our closest competitor — without spending a dollar.
The Model

Hub-and-Spoke Content Architecture

One permanent hub page per holiday targets the head keyword and gets updated annually. Spoke blog posts target long-tail keywords and link back to the hub. Together, they signal to Google that Visit Loudoun is the authority on that topic locally.

Example: Mother's Day Cluster

Mother's Day in Loudoun — Celebrate in DC's Wine Country® /things-to-do/mothers-day  ·  Head keyword: "Mother's Day Loudoun"  ·  1,000–1,500 words  ·  Updated annually
SPOKEWhere to Take Mom for Mother's Day Brunch/blog/mothers-day-brunch-loudoun
SPOKEWine Country Experiences Mom Will Love/blog/mothers-day-winery-experiences
SPOKEPlan a Mother's Day Weekend Getaway/blog/mothers-day-weekend-itinerary
SPOKELocal Gift Ideas for Mother's Day/blog/mothers-day-gift-ideas-loudoun
Why this works: The hub captures top-of-funnel searches ("Mother's Day near DC"). Each spoke ranks independently for high-intent queries ("Mother's Day brunch Loudoun"). The hub accumulates authority every year. The spokes capture traffic the hub alone can't. Together, they create a topical cluster that dominates the local search landscape.

For the Room

Questions for Discussion

These are the decisions I'd like the team's input on. I have recommendations for each, but I want to hear how these align with what everyone else is working on.

Which town pages should we tackle first?

Ashburn (73K impressions) and Leesburg (county seat, declining clicks) — but the strat plan also calls out Middleburg and eastern Loudoun. What does the team think?

Which holiday do we pilot first?

Fall Season is highest volume. Valentine's Day gives us the longest runway. Mother's Day has an existing blog post we can redirect. Which is the best pilot?

What should the Locals page look like?

The strat plan calls for community involvement and local storytelling. CTA-curated itineraries, resident-submitted recommendations, a mix? Who owns the content?

Can we redirect the 19 thin pages this month?

Quick wins that require CMS changes but no editorial work. Can we get these done in the next two weeks?

Who owns the annual holiday refresh cycle?

Each hub needs a refresh 6–8 weeks before the holiday. Should this be a recurring Asana task? Who's responsible for the annual update?

What role should the agency play?

Miles Partnership has been recommending many of these structural changes since September 2025. Should we loop them in on implementation, or handle internally first?
