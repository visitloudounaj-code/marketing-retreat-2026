Part 1 — Backstory · 5-10 minutes
The Context

Why I Looked at Other DMOs

I researched Visit Fairfax, Visit Charlottesville, Visit Napa Valley and Explore Asheville because they compete with us on three levels: they target the same keywords we do ("weekend getaways near DC," "wine country Virginia"), they have similar destination assets (wine, farms, historic towns, outdoor recreation) and they're our neighbors in the mid-Atlantic and Virginia tourism market. I wanted to see how they're structuring their websites to handle the same traffic decline every DMO is facing.

What They're Doing Differently

Visit Napa Valley

visitnapavalley.com

Gives every town its own rich hub page — Calistoga, St. Helena, Yountville — each linking to local wineries, restaurants and activities. Also has appellation pages explaining wine geography and first-time tasting tips.

Why it matters for us: Loudoun has 12 towns and communities. Our town pages average 190 words. Napa's average 1,500+.
See their Towns pages →

Explore Asheville

exploreasheville.com

Uses a pillar-cluster model: seasonal content pillars (Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter) layered on activity hubs. Blog posts feed back into hubs — not standalone content. Itineraries organized by traveler persona: "For the Romantics," "For the Pup Parent."

Why it matters for us: Our blogs and pillar pages compete with each other instead of supporting each other.
See their Things to Do structure →

Visit Fairfax

fxva.com

Segments by audience (leisure, meetings, sports, weddings) with separate content tracks. Heavy on itinerary content. However, no dedicated town pages — an area where we can leapfrog them if we build ours out.

Why it matters for us: They're our closest competitor with 3x our hotel supply. Their weakness (no town pages) is our opportunity.
See their site →

Visit Charlottesville

visitcharlottesville.org

Elevates wineries to top-level navigation (directly relevant to us). Strong blog integration into homepage. "Black Cville" section shows how niche cultural content pillars differentiate a destination. However, they also lack neighborhood sub-pages.

Why it matters for us: They won Wine Enthusiast's "Wine Region of the Year" 2023. They're directly challenging our wine identity.
See their site →

The Problem They're All Trying to Solve

AI Overviews now appear on 60 percent of travel searches and suppress click-through rates by 58–61 percent. Nearly 69 percent of all Google searches end without a click. Our own agency reports show this happening to us: organic traffic share dropped from 70 percent to 34 percent of our site traffic, and monthly clicks fell from ~34K to ~16K — even though our rankings improved from position 13 to 8.5.

The Plan

90-Day Execution Timeline

This doesn't require new budget, new tools or new hires. It's a smarter allocation of the resources we already have — redirecting our content author from blog-first to foundation-first for 90 days.

Days 1–30Prune & Redirect
  • Execute the 19 redirects
  • Rewrite meta descriptions for top 5 low-CTR pages
  • Fix duplicate title tags
  • Establish internal linking framework
  • Begin Ashburn and Leesburg town page rewrites
Days 31–60Build Town Hubs
  • Complete all 12 town pages to 1,000+ word standard
  • Expand Wine Country, Ale Trail and Horse Country pillars post-consolidation
  • Build Restaurants page with real editorial content
  • Build Locals page and Itineraries hub
  • Implement structured data markup
Days 61–90Connect & Measure
  • Link blog posts to their pillar pages (and vice versa)
  • Create Eastern/Western Loudoun regional pages
  • Resume blog publishing within the new architecture
  • Report: indexation speed, click recovery, AI citations, engagement
The sitemap audit and holiday content strategy are two pieces of one execution plan — full prioritized checklist on the Combined Action Plan page.

What I'm Asking For

Permission to redirect our content effort for 90 days — from blog-first publishing to building out the pages identified in this presentation. No new budget. No new tools. No new hires. Just a smarter allocation of the resources we already have — aligned to the Elevate Loudoun strategic plan — so that every page on our site has a purpose and every piece of future content performs dramatically better. I'll report progress at 30, 60 and 90 days.

For the Room

Questions for Discussion

These are the decisions I'd like the team's input on. I have recommendations for each, but I want to hear how these align with what everyone else is working on.

Which town pages should we tackle first?

Ashburn (73K impressions) and Leesburg (county seat, declining clicks) — but the strat plan also calls out Middleburg and eastern Loudoun. What does the team think?

Which holiday do we pilot first?

Fall Season is highest volume. Valentine's Day gives us the longest runway. Mother's Day has an existing blog post we can redirect. Which is the best pilot?

What should the Locals page look like?

The strat plan calls for community involvement and local storytelling. CTA-curated itineraries, resident-submitted recommendations, a mix? Who owns the content?

Can we redirect the 19 thin pages this month?

Quick wins that require CMS changes but no editorial work. Can we get these done in the next two weeks?

Who owns the annual holiday refresh cycle?

Each hub needs a refresh 6–8 weeks before the holiday. Should this be a recurring Asana task? Who's responsible for the annual update?

What role should the agency play?

Miles Partnership has been recommending many of these structural changes since September 2025. Should we loop them in on implementation, or handle internally first?
